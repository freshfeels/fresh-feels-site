Problem → Agitate → Solve

Section 01

What is PAS?

The simplest, most battle-tested framework in advertising.

PAS stands for Problem → Agitate → Solve. It's a three-part copywriting formula that works by meeting your audience where they already are — inside a frustration they're feeling — rather than trying to sell them something they haven't thought about yet.

Instead of leading with your product and hoping someone cares, PAS starts with the customer's pain. It names a problem they recognise, intensifies why that problem matters, then presents your product as the logical, almost inevitable solution.

P Problem

Identify the pain

Name a real frustration your audience already feels. Be specific. The more accurately you describe their problem, the more they trust you understand them.

"Most detergents are full of toxic chemicals and packaged in single-use plastic…"

A Agitate

Twist the knife

Don't just describe the problem — make it feel urgent, costly, or embarrassing. Remind them what happens if they keep ignoring it. This is what creates the emotional itch that demands to be scratched.

"…and 90% of it is just tap water you're paying premium prices for."

S Solve

Be the answer

Only after the reader feels the problem do you introduce your product. By now you're not interrupting them — you're rescuing them. The solution lands with relief rather than resistance.

"We fixed that. Our sheets are plant-based, zero plastic, and cost half the price."

Section 02

Why It Works

It's not magic — it's psychology.

People move away from pain faster than toward pleasure

Behavioural psychology consistently shows that loss aversion is a stronger motivator than potential gain. PAS leads with loss — the pain of the status quo — which activates a more powerful emotional response than leading with a benefit ever could.

It creates instant relevance

When a reader sees their exact problem described in your ad, they feel seen. That recognition creates an immediate connection — they stop scrolling because the ad feels like it was written for them, not broadcast at the world.

The solution feels earned, not sold

Most ads feel like someone trying to sell you something. PAS feels like someone helping you out of a problem. By the time your product is introduced, the reader is actively looking for the answer — so your offer arrives as a gift, not a pitch.

It works across every format

Video, static image, email, caption, spoken script — PAS is format-agnostic. Whether you have 6 seconds or 60, the three-beat structure scales. That's why it's the most durable framework in advertising.

The key insight

"Your audience isn't waiting to hear about your product. They're absorbed in their own problems. PAS meets them there first — and earns the right to introduce a solution."

"I have a confession... I LIED. I didn't think we could make our detergent sheets any cheaper, but we did. Most detergents are full of toxic chemicals ☣️ and packaged in plastic you'll never stop paying for. We fixed that — and now we've made them even more affordable."

P Problem

"Toxic chemicals and plastic packaging you can't escape"

S Solve

"We fixed that" — clean, confident, no over-explanation needed

Why it held for 290 days:

The confession hook ("I LIED") is a pattern interrupt that earns attention before the PAS framework even kicks in. Once you're watching, the problem is both environmental (chemicals) and financial (cost) — two pain points for the price of one. The solve is delivered with founder-level confidence, not feature lists.

Lucent Globe — Ad #2  ·  201 days active Evergreen

"You'll be SHOCKED to learn what's actually in your dishwashing detergent… Most brands load it with forever chemicals and 70% water — and charge you full price for the privilege. Not over here 👋 We're giving away 50% OFF the EVERYTHING BUNDLE."

P Problem

"Hidden chemicals and water — your detergent is not what you think"

A Agitate

"Charged full price for the privilege" — the industry is exploiting you

S Solve

"Not over here 👋" — positions the brand as the exception, plus immediate offer

Why it held for 201 days:

"SHOCKED" creates an information gap — a curiosity the viewer needs to close. The agitation reframes the problem as an active betrayal ("you're being taken advantage of"), not just inconvenience. This elevates the emotional stakes before the solve, making the offer feel like justice not just savings.

Section 04

How to Use It

A step-by-step process for turning a blank page into a brief-ready concept.

1

Start with the problem — not your product

Resist the urge to open with what your product does. Instead, ask: what is the customer frustrated about right now, before they've ever heard of you? Write that down in plain language, as if you're venting on their behalf.

QUESTIONS TO ASK:

  • →What do people complain about in this category on Reddit, reviews, or socials?
  • →What do they waste money on without realising it?
  • →What do they put up with because they didn't know there was a better way?
2

Make the problem feel urgent or costly

This is the step most people skip — and it's the most important one. Don't just describe the problem; describe the consequence of leaving it unsolved. What is it costing them in money, time, health, or self-image?

AGITATION TECHNIQUES:

  • →Quantify the pain: "90% of it is just water you're paying $12 for"
  • →Name the villain: "Most brands know this and charge you anyway"
  • →Project into the future: "And without changing anything, you'll keep paying for it forever"
  • →Make it personal: "You've probably already paid hundreds without realising it"
3

Introduce the solution with confidence, not desperation

Your solve should feel like relief, not a sales pitch. Keep it short. Don't list every feature. Focus on the single outcome that directly addresses the problem you named — then, optionally, layer in your offer.

SOLVE PATTERNS THAT WORK:

  • →The pivot: "Not us." / "Not anymore." / "Not over here."
  • →The simple fix: "We fixed that." / "There's a better way."
  • →The contrast: "While everyone else does X, we do Y."
4

Add a hook before the P

In video especially, you need to earn 3 seconds before the PAS framework even has a chance to land. A great hook interrupts the pattern of mindless scrolling and forces attention. Think of it as the doorbell — PAS is what's inside.

Hook types that work

  • 🎭 Confession: "I LIED about this…"
  • 😱 Shock: "You'll be SHOCKED by…"
  • ❓ Question: "Did you know your X is…?"
  • 🔢 Stat: "90% of X is just water…"
  • 🔄 Pattern break: "WORST review we ever got…"

The 3-second rule

If someone watching your video could scroll past in the first 3 seconds and miss nothing important, your hook isn't working. The hook must either promise something, reveal something, or create an information gap that demands to be filled.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping the Agitate step

Going straight from Problem to Solve without making the problem feel urgent produces weak ads. The Agitate step is what creates emotional tension — without it, the solve lands flat.

Leading with your solution

"Our product is made with natural ingredients and no plastic…" sounds like every other brand. If someone doesn't already care about the problem, they won't care about your solution. Lead with pain first.

Being vague about the problem

"Traditional cleaning products have issues" — nobody feels that. "Your detergent is 90% water and you're paying $12 for it" — everyone feels that. Specificity is what creates the "that's exactly me" moment.

Over-explaining the solution

Once the problem is felt, the viewer wants relief — not a product brochure. Keep the Solve tight: one outcome, one promise. If they want details, they'll click through.
